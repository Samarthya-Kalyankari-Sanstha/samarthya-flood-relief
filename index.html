Context, timeline & field gallery
About Samarthya & the Osmanabad flood
Women-led, youth-initiated grassroots organisation working across 50 villages in Osmanabad since 2008. The Madat programme is one of five thematic areas — all centred on the rights of De-notified and Nomadic Tribe communities.

Response timeline
September 2025
Floods hit Osmanabad. Samarthya begins rescue — 120 persons evacuated. Psychosocial support to 240 families. Coordination with district administration for loss registration and government compensation claims.
Oct – Nov 2025
Rapid Needs Assessment — 1,067 households surveyed. 97.7% reported food insecurity. 98.1% identified dry ration as priority need. Appeal launched to donors and government for support.
January 2026
Distribution begins. 1,548 kits delivered — largest single-month phase. GPS-tagged, photo-verified, with signature for every record.
February 2026
1,272 kits distributed. Coverage expanded across remaining habitations in Omerga and Lohara blocks.
March 2026
Final 180 kits distributed. 111-household Post-Distribution Monitoring survey completed. Operation verified and closed.

"Seeds, livelihoods, and soil restoration." This is what 111 households told us they needed next, in the April 2026 Post-Distribution Monitoring. This page is what we are doing about it.
Why this, why now
Problem 1 · Climate
What the relief data showed
Complete crop loss (soybean, onion, pulses)78 of 111 PDM respondents
Irrigation on participating plotsNone, fully rain-dependent
Single-woman-headed households53.15% of relief beneficiaries
One failed monsoon away from crisis. Marginal rainfed plots carry the region's climate risk with the fewest buffers.
Problem 2 · Nutrition
The crisis behind the crisis
Anaemia & undernutritionWidespread among women, girls & children
Cash-crop-first cultivationFew vegetables, pulses & millets on the plate
Who eats lastThe women who grow the food
Hidden hunger. When rainfed crops fail, dietary diversity is cut first, and women, adolescent girls and children are affected first.
One answer. Climate risk and hidden hunger are the same problem. We are answering both with one intervention: climate-resilient farming that puts nutrient-rich food back on the plate, led by the women who grow it.
The project
Women-Led Climate Resilient Farming for Food and Dignity
One-year women-led recovery initiative
Women play a central role in farming across the habitations, yet are rarely recognised as farmers or decision-makers. Over twelve months spanning the kharif and rabi seasons, 150 women, many of them single women cultivating small rainfed plots, will lead the recovery of their own food systems: sowing bio-fortified and traditional seed, farming organically, planting fruit trees on their bunds, building a community seed bank, and putting diverse, nutrient-rich food back on the family plate.

🌾 Bio-fortified seed for rainfed crops
Introducing bio-fortified and improved varieties of jowar, bajra, soybean, redgram, greengram, horsegram, groundnut, lentil and other pulses: micronutrient-dense staples suited to rain-only plots.
NutritionClimate
🪱 Integrated Soil & Pest Management (INM & IPDM)
Combining organic nutrient management (bio-fertilizers, compost, green manure) with natural pest control (bio-pesticides, traps, border crops) to restore soils damaged by floods while cutting input costs.
ClimateEcology
🥬 Household Nutrition Gardens & Bund Horticulture
Establishing diverse vegetable kitchen gardens alongside fruit-bearing trees planted directly on farm bunds. This integration ensures daily dietary diversity, future income, and active soil conservation on the same square foot of land.
